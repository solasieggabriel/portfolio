Minimum Colors for Non-perfect Coloring of the Eleven Euclidean Uniform Tilings

Author: Sieg Gabriel Sola Coauthors: K.N.L., M.F.P. Adviser: K.L., PhD

ABSTRACT

This study investigates the relationship between color symmetry and geometric structure of the 11 Euclidean uniform tilings. A coloring is considered perfect if the tiling's symmetries preserve the color pattern through transformations. The primary objective of this study is to determine the minimum number of colors required to guarantee non-perfect coloring where symmetry is necessarily broken for each tiling. The methodology utilizes group theory, specifically analyzing transitive tile orbits and stabilizer subgroups. Orbits are classified as Type I, those who share the symmetries of regular tilings, and otherwise Type II, requiring a derived index formula based on point group and translation subgroup indices. By generating the complete set of perfect coloring numbers for each tiling, the minimum non-perfect coloring number is identified as the smallest integer not present in that set. Results show that regular tilings require minimal colors (2 or 3) to enforce non-perfection. However, tilings with multiple orbits require significantly more. For example, the 4.8.8 tiling requires 7 colors, while the 3.6.3.6 and 3.12.12 tilings both require 23 colors to ensure a non-perfect coloring. These findings quantify the threshold at which color symmetry is lost and provide a structured framework for analyzing non-symmetry in mathematical patterns.

Introduction to Color Symmetries

Geometric symmetry has long served as a pillar for understanding natural patterns, crystallography, and tessellations. When colors are introduced to a tiling, the symmetry operations can behave in one of two distinct ways: either they preserve the color permutation (resulting in a perfect coloring), or they break the color pattern (yielding a non-perfect coloring).

While historical literature focuses heavily on classifying perfect colorings, this study explores the opposite limit. We seek to answer the following question: What is the absolute minimum number of colors required to guarantee that a coloring of a given uniform tiling is non-perfect?

Figure 1. Perfect coloring (left) vs. non-perfect coloring (right) of the regular tiling 4.4.4.4 with symmetry group G = p4m.

As illustrated in Figure 1 above, a standard checkerboard pattern on the regular square tiling (4.4.4.4) constitutes a perfect 2-coloring because every symmetry operation of the grid maps red tiles to red tiles (or swaps red and green systematically). However, a different layout of the same colors on the right breaks certain reflection and rotation symmetries, making it non-perfect.

Because it is still possible to color the square tiling perfectly using 2 colors, 2 colors do not guarantee non-perfection. We must look higher in the integer set to find the threshold where perfect colorings become group-theoretically impossible.

Group-Theoretic Methodology

To determine these thresholds, we model each uniform tiling using its transitive tile orbits under its respective planar symmetry group G. Under this framework, tile orbits are categorized into two primary types:

  • Type I Orbits: Orbits that share the symmetries of the underlying regular tilings. These grids exhibit high symmetry and have relatively small stabilizer subgroups.
  • Type II Orbits: Orbits that require a derived index formula based on point group and translation subgroup indices. These orbits have more complex configurations and symmetry-breaking paths.

Key Findings & Numerical Thresholds

Our analysis reveals that the threshold at which color symmetry is lost is directly correlated to the number of transitive tile orbits and the complexity of the stabilizers:

  1. Regular Tilings: Tilings with a single orbit (e.g., 4.4.4.4, 3.3.3.3.3.3, 6.6.6) require very few colors (typically 2 or 3) to enforce non-perfection.
  2. Archimedean Tilings: Tilings with multiple orbits exhibit a dramatic surge in complexity. The 4.8.8 tiling requires a minimum of 7 colors to guarantee non-perfection.
  3. Extreme Cases: The highly complex 3.6.3.6 (kagome lattice) and 3.12.12 tilings require a minimum of 23 colors to ensure a non-perfect coloring layout.

Conclusion and Applications

By proving these numerical thresholds, this study establishes a mathematical bridge between color group theory and physical materials research. In crystallography and quantum gate spacing, identifying the point of guaranteed non-symmetry helps define boundaries for physical stability and structural spacing parameters. Future extensions of this model will explore non-perfect bounds on hyperbolic lattices.
