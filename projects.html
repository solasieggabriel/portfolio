PURE.MATHEMATICS DEC 2025

Minimum Colors for Non-perfect Coloring of the Eleven Euclidean Uniform Tilings

ABSTRACT

This study investigates the relationship between color symmetry and geometric structure of the 11 Euclidean uniform tilings. A coloring is considered perfect if the tiling's symmetries preserve the color pattern through transformations. The primary objective of this study is to determine the minimum number of colors required to guarantee non-perfect coloring where symmetry is necessarily broken for each tiling. The methodology utilizes group theory, specifically analyzing transitive tile orbits and stabilizer subgroups. Orbits are classified as Type I, those who share the symmetries of regular tilings, and otherwise Type II, requiring a derived index formula based on point group and translation subgroup indices. By generating the complete set of perfect coloring numbers for each tiling, the minimum non-perfect coloring number is identified as the smallest integer not present in that set. Results show that regular tilings require minimal colors (2 or 3) to for non-perfection. However, tilings with multiple orbits require significantly more. For example, the 4.8.8 tiling requires 7 colors, while the 3.6.3.6 and 3.12.12 tilings both require 23 colors to ensire a non-perfect coloring. These findings quantify the threshold at which color symmetry is lost and provide a structured framework for analyzing non-symmetry in mathematical patters.

Coauthors: K.N.L., M.F.P.  |  Adviser: K.L., PhD
Figure 1. Perfect coloring (left) vs. non-perfect coloring (right) of the regular tiling 4.4.4.4 with symmetry group G = p4m.

Figure 1 illustrates two distinct 2-colorings of the regular 4.4.4.4 tiling, whose full symmetry group is G = p4m. On the left, the checkerboard pattern represents a perfect coloring, where every symmetry operation of the uncolored tiling preserves or cleanly permutes the color distribution. On the right yields a non-perfect coloring, where certain original symmetries (such as specific reflections or rotations) are broken by the color arrangement. Importantly, while 2 colors are sufficient to produce a non-perfect coloring here, 2 colors do not represent the absolute minimum color bound that forces or guarantees non-perfection for this tiling. Because a 2-color allocation can still result in a perfect coloring (left), using 2 colors does not guarantee symmetry-breaking. The core goal of this study is to determine the minimum number of colors required to ensure non-perfection for each of the 11 Euclidean uniform tilings.

APPLIED.STATISTICS FEB 2026

Evaluating 14-Day Ahead Weather Predictions in the City of Manila: A Comparative Multivariate Study of ARIMAX and Lagged Multiple Linear Regression

ABSTRACT

This study presents a comparative evaluation of Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average with Exogenous Regressors (ARIMAX) and Lagged Multiple Linear Regression models for predicting meteorological variables in Manila. Differentiating predictive accuracy and error propagation for temperature, wind speed, and humidity, we leverage historical weather datasets and exogenous predictors. Results show that while ARIMAX excels in modeling short-term temporal dependencies, Lagged Multiple Linear Regression shows robust stability over longer horizons.

Coauthors: J.D.A., R.M.S., & 4 others  |  Adviser: K.L., PhD
Figure 1. Comparison of predicted vs. actual daily temperature profiles in Manila, contrasting ARIMAX (green) and Lagged MLR (gold) projections over a 14-day forecast window.

Our analysis provides a quantitative comparison of the error accumulation curves for both forecasting frameworks. By mapping exogenous parameters such as solar radiation and barometric pressure, we show the threshold where lagged dependencies decay, offering a structured modeling foundation for localized meteorological intelligence.
