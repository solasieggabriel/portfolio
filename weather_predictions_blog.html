Evaluating 14-Day Ahead Weather Predictions in the City of Manila: A Comparative Multivariate Study of ARIMAX and Lagged Multiple Linear Regression

Author: Sieg Gabriel Sola Coauthors: J.D.A., R.M.S., & 4 others Adviser: K.L., PhD

ABSTRACT

This study presents a comparative evaluation of Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average with Exogenous Regressors (ARIMAX) and Lagged Multiple Linear Regression models for predicting meteorological variables in Manila. Differentiating predictive accuracy and error propagation for temperature, wind speed, and humidity, we leverage historical weather datasets and exogenous predictors. Results show that while ARIMAX excels in modeling short-term temporal dependencies, Lagged Multiple Linear Regression shows robust stability over longer horizons.

Introduction to Meteorological Forecasting

Predicting weather patterns in dense tropical areas like the City of Manila presents a series of statistical hurdles. Rapid microclimatic variations, high humidity index coefficients, and localized pressure drops mean that classical forecast systems face high rate errors beyond 48 hours.

In this comparative study, we model 14-day ahead meteorological trends utilizing two multivariate frameworks: ARIMAX (a linear time-series extension utilizing exogenous parameters) and **Lagged Multiple Linear Regression** (MLR, incorporating lagged meteorological predictors). Our study focuses on temperature dynamics, solar radiation thresholds, and relative humidity indices.

Figure 1. Comparison of predicted vs. actual daily temperature profiles in Manila, contrasting ARIMAX (green) and Lagged MLR (gold) projections over a 14-day forecast window.

As illustrated in Figure 1, both statistical models show different strengths across the forecast window. ARIMAX captures the micro-fluctuations (autoregressive components) during the initial 3–5 days, yielding a tight predictive fit. However, as the projection horizon extends toward 14 days, the model's error propagation expands significantly. Conversely, the Lagged MLR model stabilizes quickly, maintaining consistent mean absolute error bands across the entire window.

Methodology and Data Engineering

We sourced historical weather metrics for Manila from open-source weather archives (including solar radiation, barometric pressure, ambient temperature, and precipitation data). The statistical modeling was executed as follows:

  • ARIMAX Setup: A $(p, d, q)$ order configuration was optimized using AIC sweeps, incorporating exogenous variables (solar radiation and humidity) to capture thermodynamic heat inputs.
  • Lagged MLR Setup: Independent variables were structured using lag shifts of 1, 3, and 7 days. Exogenous parameters were filtered for multicollinearity using Variance Inflation Factors (VIF < 5.0) before model entry.
"ARIMAX shows superior short-term precision due to autoregressive adjustment, but Lagged Multiple Linear Regression remains structurally superior for longer horizons due to lower error variance propagation."

Statistical Results

Our quantitative models indicate key differences in prediction boundaries and error profiles:

  1. Short-Term Horizon (Days 1–3): ARIMAX outperformed Lagged MLR by 12.4% in Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) for temperature due to localized residual correction loops.
  2. Mid-Term Horizon (Days 4–7): The models perform comparably, with overlapping confidence intervals.
  3. Long-Term Horizon (Days 8–14): Error parameters for ARIMAX grow exponentially as cumulative lags pile up. Lagged Multiple Regression shows stable, linear bounds, outperforming ARIMAX by 8.5% in temperature prediction accuracy.

Conclusion

Our research indicates that the ideal meteorology forecasting framework is a hybrid configuration. Short-range forecasts should prioritize ARIMAX loops to capture autocorrelation, while extended forecasts (beyond 7 days) must rely on Lagged Multiple Linear Regression constraints to prevent structural drift. Future research will explore integrating machine learning estimators to optimize seasonal lag thresholds.
